Marie Bridge

Nowaday, the Marie bridge is 22 m wide and 100 m long. It was build by Christophe Marie, hence the bridge’s name, between 1614 and 1630. Louis XIII laid the cornerstone. It consist of 5 stones arches. Above the starlings, four stone niches located upstream and downstream were meant to shelter statues which were never placed.

History:

Build between the Hôtel-de-Ville dock and the Bourbon dock, located in the Île Saint-Louis, this bridge is 15 m wide and 160 m long. It was built in 1860-1861 by Romany and Savarin. It has three elliptical arches of 32 m. It has replaced the previous suspension bridge, which was built almost at the same place by the Seguin brothers in 1833. The bridge was supported by a portico, located at the south end of the island. It connected the right bank to the Cité island, and ended near the Flower market. Registered work. Construction: 1928-1930

Auteur:

Histoire:

The site of the bridge of the Tournelle knew many successive works to cross the Seine. In the Middle Ages, there was a wooden bridge, half excited by a flood on January 21, 1651, which was rebuilt in stone in 1656. Demolished in 1918, it was replaced by the current bridge in 1928. The term "Tournelle" comes from the presence in this place in the twelfth century of a turret of the walls of Philippe Auguste replaced by a small castle. The St. Bernard Gate is to the south of this castle.

The bridge of the Tournelle, 120m long, 22m wide, built in 1928. It is a bridge in reinforced concrete covered with stone. The present bridge is at the site of older works. In 1369, a wooden bridge connected the left bank to Notre-Dame Island. In 1618-1620, the contractor Christophe Marie rebuilt it to favor the operations of subdivision of the island. The bridge was made of stone only in 1654-1656, then modified, it had six arches. The present bridge includes a large central low arch and two semicircular lateral arches. Unregistered work.

The bridge, or more exactly the Sully bridges, because there is two autonomous bridges connecting Boulevard Henri IV and boulevard Saint-Germain. hey were built in 1864-1866. These metallic bridges made by engineers Vaudrey and Brosselin have three arches on the large arm - right bank - an arch on the small arm.

Before the construction of the Sully Bridge; Upstream of Île Saint-Louis there were two footbridges: the footbridge of Damietta on the right bank, the footbridge of Constantine on the left bank. A pedestrian bridge over a wooden boom joined the upstream tip of the island to the Quai Henri IV. Several times built, it disappeared definitively from the Parisian landscape only in 1933. This estacade often represented by the painters was intended to protect from ice drift the boats parked in winter at the port of the Célectins-Saint Paul, on the small arm Of the Seine.

The square which occupies the point of the island and the median between the two bridges constituted in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries a part of the french garden of the Hotel de Bretonvilliers, destroyed in 1840. Work inscribed.

Saint-Louis Bridges

Linking the “Île de la Cité” to “Île Saint-Louis”, the Saint-Louis’s bridge was rebuilt in 1970, replacing a temporary bridge installed in 1941. It is a single-arch metal work, without aesthetic qualities.

Plus d'informations

Linking the “Île de la Cité” to “Île Saint-Louis”, the Saint-Louis’s bridge was rebuilt in 1970, replacing a temporary bridge installed in 1941. It is a single-arch metal work, without aesthetic qualities.

The first bridge linking the City to Île Saint-Louis was built in the 17th century, when Île Saint-Louis began to be inhabited. It was a wooden footbridge built in 1627. Rebuilt in 1710, it took the name of "Red Bridge" because of the red paint minium of which it was covered. A new bridge was built at the beginning of the Empire. In 1842, it was replaced by a suspension bridge. In 1861, a new metal work was built with a single arch of 64m.
